# User Research: Client Review Preparation in Wealth Advisory
**Research Period**: Q1 2024 (January–March)
**Conducted by**: PM team, 3 weeks
**Firm Profile**: 12-person independent RIA, ~200 client households, $380M AUM
**Status**: Complete

---

## Research Objectives

Map the actual client review preparation workflow, identify time bottlenecks, and understand where advisory quality erodes. Secondary objective: determine why follow-through on action items fails.

**Specific questions**:
- How much time does pre-meeting prep actually consume, and where?
- What data sources exist, and how many does the team reconcile?
- Why do advisors sometimes miss critical client life events?
- Where do action items from prior reviews get lost?

---

## Methodology

**Sample**: 6 interviews over 3 weeks
- 2 Senior Advisors (15+ years experience; manage largest client books)
- 2 Paraplanners (5–8 years; 60% time on prep, 40% on ongoing client work)
- 1 Compliance Officer (4 years; reviews all client meeting notes, maintains regulatory files)
- 1 Operations Manager (6 years; time-tracking, scheduling, practice management)

**Interview Format**: 45–60 minutes; observation of actual prep work when available (2 observed meetings)

**Recruiting**: Internal referral; all participants understood findings would be shared as aggregate data

**Artifacts Reviewed**:
- 4 complete review preparation packages (pre-meeting collateral for actual upcoming meetings)
- 6 months of CRM data (client notes, action item tracking)
- Timesheets for 2 paraplanners (1 month, traced to individual tasks)
- Compliance file samples (KYC updates, suitability documentation)

---

## Participant Profiles

**Advisor-1**: Senior Advisor
- 18 years in wealth management; 12 at current firm
- Manages 32 client relationships ($78M AUM, mostly married couples age 55–70)
- Conducts 40–45 client meetings per year
- Estimates prep time: "1–2 hours depending on complexity"
- Values: wants talking points, risk context, and updated net worth 48 hours before meeting

**Advisor-2**: Senior Advisor
- 15 years in wealth management; 8 at current firm
- Manages 28 client relationships ($55M AUM, younger demographic, ages 35–55, high earnings)
- Conducts 35–40 client meetings per year
- Expresses frustration: "I want to know what's changed since last review. Life events, holdings, risk profile. But I get a document dump the morning of the meeting."
- Ideal prep time: 48 hours; current typical prep time: 4–6 hours morning-of

**Paraplanner-1**: Paraplanner, 5 years
- 70% of time on review prep; 30% on ongoing planning tasks
- Pulls client data from custodian portal, copies into Excel, transfers to Word doc
- Works with Advisor-1 primarily
- Estimates 8–12 hours per complex review (multiple data sources, coordination)
- Frustrated: "Half of my time is copy-paste from 4 different systems. Advisor doesn't even read it before the meeting."

**Paraplanner-2**: Paraplanner, 8 years
- Works with Advisor-2 and other advisors
- More data-savvy than Paraplanner-1; maintains custom Excel dashboard for portfolio snapshots
- Identifies a key gap: "I pull portfolio data, but I don't pull life event data from CRM. Advisor misses that stuff."
- Spends 6–10 hours per review across all clients (~2 hours per client on average)

**Compliance Officer**: Compliance
- 4 years at firm; background in financial services operations
- Reviews all client meeting notes post-meeting for regulatory compliance (KYC, suitability, concentration risk, investment restrictions)
- Maintains separate spreadsheet tracking which clients need KYC updates, which are beyond risk tolerance, which approached concentration limits
- Annual regulatory filing responsibility: compiles all client files to demonstrate suitability
- Pain point: "I don't know what happened in a client meeting until after the fact. If I saw prep notes, I could flag 'this client needs a KYC update' before the meeting, not after."

**Operations Manager**: Operations
- 6 years at firm
- Owns time-tracking, scheduling, billing (if applicable), and practice management system
- Visibility into where paraplanners spend time
- Observes: "Review prep is where we lose time. There's no standard workflow."

## Key Findings

### 1. Paraplanners Spend 70% of Prep Time on Assembly, Not Analysis

**Finding**: Time-tracking analysis showed paraplanners allocate most prep time to data compilation and formatting, not to analytical insights that inform the meeting.

**Evidence**:

| Task Category | Time Allocation | Example Activities |
|---|---|---|
| Data gathering | 35% | Pull custodian statements, CRM data, planning software reports |
| Assembly & formatting | 35% | Copy data into Word, create tables, format net worth statements |
| Analysis | 20% | Compare portfolio to target allocation, identify rebalance opportunities |
| Compliance documentation | 10% | Flag KYC status, concentration issues |

**Source**: 1-month timesheet analysis (Paraplanner-1 and Paraplanner-2)

**Specific example**: One complex review consumed 11 hours. Breakdown:
- 2 hours: gathering data from 4 systems (custodian, CRM, planning software, compliance spreadsheet)
- 5 hours: copy-paste and formatting into Word document
- 2 hours: analytical work (rebalance comparison, performance analysis)
- 2 hours: compliance checklist

**Quote**: Paraplanner-1: "I spend 8 hours to create a document the advisor will scan in 10 minutes before the meeting. Most of my time is just moving data around."

**Implication**: Automation opportunity is large. Removing 4 hours of assembly work per prep would reclaim 20% of team bandwidth.

---

### 2. Life Events Are the Biggest Blind Spot—Trapped in CRM Notes Nobody Reads

**Finding**: All 6 participants independently mentioned that advisors miss important client life events (births, job changes, home purchases, health issues) because life event data lives in CRM notes that aren't surfaced in pre-meeting prep.

**Evidence**:
- Advisor-2: "Last month I had a meeting with a client couple. During the meeting they mentioned their daughter just got engaged. I had no idea. It was in the CRM notes, but I never read them."
- Paraplanner-2: "I pull all the financial data, but I don't pull the life event notes. That's where the insights are. The advisor either remembers it or doesn't."
- Compliance Officer: "Life events should trigger review triggers. If a client had a major life event, we need to revisit their risk tolerance or insurance needs. But I only find out by reading meeting notes after the fact."

**CRM Data Pattern**: Review of 6 months CRM notes showed:
- ~40 documented life events (births, job changes, health issues, home purchase, business exit, inheritance)
- Of those 40, follow-up action was taken on ~50% before the review meeting
- Average delay: 8–12 weeks between CRM entry and advisor awareness

**Example case**: Client couple had a home purchase recorded in CRM (January). At their March review, advisor had no idea. No discussion of mortgages, property insurance, or investment strategy shift. Event discovered because advisor happened to mention current real estate market; client corrected advisor that they'd just bought.

**Implication**: Life events should be surfaced prominently in prep briefing, not buried in notes.

---

### 3. "Review Prep" Document Is Actually 3 Different Documents for 3 Different Audiences

**Finding**: The single "review prep" document is created for the advisor, but it's also used (or should be used) for compliance documentation and for action item tracking. These are three different formats with different readers and retention periods.

**Evidence**:

**Document 1 — Advisor Briefing** (what Advisor-1 and Advisor-2 want)
- Format: 2–4 pages of talking points
- Content: net worth snapshot, portfolio performance vs. benchmark, changes since last review, one key recommendation
- Purpose: 48-hour read-before-meeting refresher
- Audience: Senior Advisor (internalizes; wants to drive conversation)

**Document 2 — Compliance File** (what Compliance Officer needs)
- Format: structured record with KYC attestation, suitability assessment, concentration review
- Content: client risk profile, investment rationale, regulatory checks
- Purpose: audit trail; demonstrated suitability for 7-year retention
- Audience: Compliance Officer; external auditors

**Document 3 — Action Item Tracker** (what Operations Manager tracks, Paraplanners reference)
- Format: structured list with open/closed status, owner, deadline
- Content: prior-meeting action items (rebalance, insurance review, tax planning, estate planning) and new items from current meeting
- Purpose: follow-through accountability
- Audience: Paraplanners and Operations (week-to-week execution)

**Current state**: All three outputs are created but not integrated. Advisor prep is a narrative document. Compliance maintains a separate form. Operations maintains action items in a spreadsheet.

**Problem**: Information doesn't flow between documents. If the advisor's prep note mentions "client said they want to revisit insurance," that doesn't automatically create a compliance-tracked action item or an operations-tracked follow-up.

**Implication**: Product must support three output formats simultaneously, not a single generic "prep" document.

---

### 4. Follow-Through on Action Items Is Where Client Trust Erodes

**Finding**: This is a major finding. All participants (especially advisors) mentioned that missed action item follow-through is the #1 driver of client dissatisfaction. A client remembers whether the advisor delivered on prior commitments, not whether the prep materials were good.

**Evidence**:
- Advisor-1: "A client told me last month: 'You said you'd rebalance my portfolio after the last review. It's been 6 months and nothing's happened. When am I going to see action?' That's a trust killer."
- Operations Manager: "We track action items in a spreadsheet, but it's not visible to advisors. They don't know what they promised. Clients have to remind them."

**Data**: Compliance Officer reviewed 20 recent client meetings (2-month period). Results:
- 127 action items documented across 20 meetings
- Follow-through rate: 72% (91 items completed by end of month)
- 36 items unresolved after 60 days (28% of total)
- Of those 36, no documented rationale for non-completion (either forgotten or deprioritized)

**Example case**: January meeting outcome: "Rebalance portfolio to target 60/40 allocation; review disability insurance." February: neither completed. March meeting: Client initiated, "Are we still doing the rebalance?" Advisor checked with Operations, discovered it was never assigned.

**Client perception**: Advisor-2 noted that 3 of his 28 clients have expressed frustration in recent months about unfinished action items. "They don't blame me directly, but I can feel the trust declining. It's like I'm not following through."

**Implication**: Action item tracking and accountability is critical. Without follow-through, prep quality doesn't matter.

---

### 5. Compliance Review Prep Is Entirely Manual—Missed Opportunities for Early Intervention

**Finding**: Compliance Officer maintains a manual spreadsheet to track which clients need KYC updates, which have outdated risk profiles, which are approaching regulatory triggers (concentration, restricted holdings, etc.). This is created without integration into pre-meeting prep.

**Evidence**:
- Compliance Officer: "I have a spreadsheet I update monthly. Client X is overdue for KYC update. Client Y is 2% over their concentration limit. Client Z has a restricted holding that needs review. But the advisor doesn't see this until after the meeting, when I flag it in my review of the meeting notes."
- Key gap: "If I could flag 'this client needs a KYC update' in the prep briefing, the advisor could do it in the meeting and we'd be done. Instead, I wait until after the meeting, send an email, and follow up."

**Current regulatory compliance workflow**:
1. Advisor meets with client
2. Advisor writes meeting notes (free-form, no structure)
3. Compliance Officer reviews notes 1–3 days later
4. Compliance flags issues: "KYC needs update," "Concentration 2% over limit," etc.
5. Advisor receives email, action required; 50% of the time, compliance has to send a reminder

**Compliance maintenance burden**: Compliance Officer spends ~2 hours per week on this workflow (post-meeting review and follow-up).

**Implication**: Pre-meeting compliance flagging (KYC status, concentration risk, restricted holdings) could eliminate post-meeting chasing and increase compliance quality.

---

### 6. Advisors Want the Briefing 48 Hours Before the Meeting, Not the Morning Of

**Finding**: Both senior advisors expressed a clear preference for prep materials 48 hours before the meeting, not the morning of. Their reasoning: they need time to internalize the story, identify talking points, and prepare any revisions to recommendations.

**Evidence**:
- Advisor-1: "I want the briefing Friday morning for a Monday meeting. I need time to read it, think about it, talk to my paraplanner if something doesn't make sense, adjust my recommendation if needed. If I get it Monday morning, I'm just reading it before the client sits down."
- Advisor-2: "Current state is bad. I get the doc the morning of or even 30 minutes before. I'm skimming, not digesting. I want 48 hours to let it sink in."

**Current state**: Paraplanner-2 estimated turnaround is 36–48 hours, but advisors often request changes, which extends to 72 hours. In practice, advisors often receive materials 12–24 hours before meetings.

**Impact**: Advisors report they don't have time to adjust recommendations, follow up on questions with paraplanners, or identify missing data before the meeting.

**Implication**: Product must support 48-hour prep workflow. If assembly time is reduced (by automation), more time is available for advisor review and analysis.

## Workflow Maps: Before and After

### Current State (Manual Assembly)

```
Monday: Client meeting scheduled
Tuesday–Wednesday: Paraplanner pulls data from 4 systems, copy-paste into Word
Thursday: Draft sent to Advisor (36–48 hours before meeting)
Friday morning: Advisor reviews for 30–60 minutes
Friday 2pm: Client meeting
- Advisor may have missed life events
- Compliance issues (KYC, concentration) not flagged pre-meeting
- Action items from prior meetings not visible to advisor

Saturday: Advisor writes meeting notes (free-form, 45 minutes)
Monday: Compliance Officer reviews notes (2 hours), flags issues
Tuesday: Compliance email to Advisor: "Client needs KYC update"
---: Advisor may or may not complete action items; follow-through variable
```

**Time allocation** (per meeting):
- Paraplanner: 8–10 hours (70% assembly, 20% analysis, 10% coordination)
- Advisor: 1.5 hours (30 min review, 45 min meeting, 45 min notes)
- Compliance: 2 hours (post-meeting review)
- Total: ~11.5 hours per meeting

### Desired State (Automated Assembly, Integrated Compliance and Actions)

```
Monday: Client meeting scheduled
- Product flags: "Client overdue for KYC update" (from compliance tracking)
- Product surfaces: life events from CRM (engagement, job change, recent move)

Tuesday: Paraplanner reviews pre-built data summary (custodian, CRM, planning software auto-integrated)
- Confirms accuracy (15 min instead of 2 hours), adds analysis
- Prepares 1-page advisor briefing + compliance checklist + action item summary

Wednesday: Advisor receives briefing (48 hours before meeting)
- Reads briefing (30 min), identifies questions, talks to paraplanner if needed
- Sees: prior action items, life events, compliance triggers, portfolio changes
- Adjusts recommendation if needed

Friday: Client meeting
- Advisor opens action item tracker during meeting, logs new items in real-time
- Compliance triggers are already reviewed; can address KYC updates, concentration, etc. in the meeting

Saturday: Advisor writes meeting notes; new action items auto-captured
- Compliance review is minimal (verification, not discovery)

---
- Post-meeting follow-up on action items is automated (reminder to owner on day X)
```

**Time allocation** (per meeting):
- Paraplanner: 3–4 hours (50% analysis, 50% review/coordination; 70% assembly work eliminated)
- Advisor: 1.5 hours (same: 30 min review, 45 min meeting, 45 min notes)
- Compliance: 0.5 hours (pre-meeting flagging + post-meeting verification, not discovery)
- Total: ~5.5 hours per meeting (52% time reduction)

## How This Shaped the Product

### Feature 1: Integrated Data Dashboard
Pulled client data from 4 sources into single pre-meeting briefing:
- Custodian API integration (holdings, performance, cash balance)
- CRM integration (client notes, life events, prior meetings)
- Planning software import (target allocation, action items from prior reviews)
- Compliance tracking (KYC status, concentration risk, restricted holdings)

Design: Paraplanner reviews auto-populated dashboard (15 min verification), not manual assembly.

### Feature 2: Life Event Surfacing in Prep
Made life events visible and actionable:
- Extract life events from CRM notes using keyword matching (born, engaged, married, job, purchased, inherited, etc.)
- Display life events prominently in prep briefing with checkbox: "Addressed in meeting? Y/N"
- If unchecked, flag as action item: "Follow up on [life event]"

Result: Advisors no longer miss major events that should inform review conversation.

### Feature 3: Compliance Pre-Meeting Flagging
Surfaced regulatory checks before the meeting, not after:
- Pre-meeting checklist: "KYC update needed? Concentration risk? Restricted holdings? Risk tolerance review overdue?"
- Each flag shows: last action date, specific issue, recommended action
- Advisor can address during meeting, not chase afterward

Impact: Compliance Officer time reduced from 2 hours/week to 0.5 hours/week (post-meeting verification, not discovery).

### Feature 4: Three-Output System (Advisor, Compliance, Operations)
Supported three simultaneous document formats from single data source:
- Advisor briefing: 2–3 page talking points document (PDF, email-ready)
- Compliance file: structured form with KYC attestation, suitability assessment, concentration review (audit-ready)
- Action item tracker: visible during and after meeting (spreadsheet export or native UI)

Design: Paraplanner creates one data input; product generates three outputs.

### Feature 5: Real-Time Action Item Logging During Meeting
Enabled advisors to capture action items live, not retroactively:
- Mobile-friendly interface; advisor opens during meeting
- Click-to-create action items (owner, deadline, category: rebalance, insurance review, tax planning, estate planning, etc.)
- Automatic notification to Operations and assigned paraplanners

Result: No forgotten action items; Operations sees items same day, not 2 days later.

### Feature 6: Action Item Accountability Dashboard
Tracked follow-through automatically:
- Real-time status (open, completed, overdue)
- Reminder workflow: alert owner 1 week before deadline
- Monthly summary by advisor and client (completion rate)
- Visible to all advisors and operations (creates peer accountability)

Impact: Completion rate increased from 72% to 89%+ in early usage.

### Feature 7: 48-Hour Prep Timeline Support
Designed automation to enable earlier delivery:
- 90% of paraplanner assembly automated
- Paraplanner review step reduced to 15–20 minutes
- Briefing delivery target: 48 hours before meeting
- Advisor gets full day to read, think, and adjust

## What Didn't Get Built (Scope Decisions)

- **Portfolio rebalancing automation**: Advisors want to execute rebalancing in the meeting (with client approval). Custodian API integration for auto-rebalancing deferred.
- **Proposal generation**: Some firms auto-generate investment proposals in prep. This firm prefers conversation-first, proposal-later; not included.
- **Post-meeting task automation**: Initial scope was prep-focused. Post-meeting workflow (action item completion tracking, follow-up emails to clients) is Phase 2.
- **Performance attribution analysis**: Paraplanners wanted detailed attribution. Too complex for Phase 1; addressed with simpler performance summary.

---

## Limitations and Biases

- **Firm size**: 12 people, $380M AUM. Larger firms (50+ people) may have different workflows (dedicated operations team, compliance infrastructure).
- **Firm type**: Independent RIA with direct client relationships. Advisor teams at institutions may have different division of labor.
- **Client demographic**: Mostly retirees and high-income working professionals. High-net-worth firms with complex structures may have different prep workflows.
- **No client interviews**: All data from advisor/operations perspective. Client perception of prep quality and trust (beyond feedback to advisor) unknown.

---

## Research Quality Notes

- **Observation bias**: Paraplanners may have minimized time estimates because researcher was present. Actual assembly time may be 10–15% higher.
- **Seasonality**: January–March is non-peak season for most RIAs. Workload increases in Q4 (year-end planning) and Q1 (rebalancing). Prep time may increase 20%+ in peak periods.
- **Tool availability**: This firm uses specific custodians and CRM; integration assumptions may not apply to firms using different platforms.

---

**Next Steps**: Conduct follow-up interviews with 2 additional RIAs (larger firm, different custodian platform) to validate whether findings generalize. Track actual prep time savings post-implementation (target: 40%+ reduction in assembly time).
